Copyright Policy

Copyright is a form of protection granted to the creators of original works, such as literary, dramatic,
musical, and artistic creations, as soon as they are fixed in a tangible medium. It provides creators
with exclusive rights to reproduce, distribute, perform, display, and create derivative works based on
their original creations. Generally, this protection lasts for the life of the author plus an additional 70 years,
although this can vary depending on the type of work and jurisdiction. Copyright protection is automatic,
but registering a work can offer additional legal benefits. Creators have both moral rights, which protect
their personal connection to their work, and economic rights, which allow them to profit from their creations.
There are limitations and exceptions to copyright, such as fair use, which allows for limited use of copyrighted
material without permission for purposes like criticism, commentary, news reporting, teaching, scholarship,
and research. Unauthorized use of copyrighted material constitutes infringement, which can lead to legal
consequences including fines and damages. Understanding these elements helps ensure that creators'
rights are protected and their works are used appropriately.

1. Understand what is copyrightable

Not all works are protected by copyright laws. Usually, original pieces such as paintings, photographs,
and digital outputs like graphic designs and computer programs are protected.

2. Know the length of copyright protection

Understanding the duration of copyright protection can be very beneficial. For most works created after 1977,
protection lasts for the author's life, followed by an additional 70 years.

3. Use watermarks and copyright notices

Adding copyright notices or watermarks to your digital art can discourage misuse of your work and is
a clear sign that you are claiming copyright over the piece.

4. Understanding “fair use”

Certain legal exceptions, known as “fair use,” permit using copyrighted works without violating copyright
laws. These exceptions typically include teaching, scholarship, research, criticism, commentary, and news
reporting. These don't violate copyright, so it's essential to familiarize yourself with them.

5. Proof of creation / ownership

If you can prove that you are the original creator of the digital artwork, that can serve as legal proof if
a dispute ever arises. An example of proof would be a layered working file, a creation video such as a
speedpaint, or livestreams where the artwork is created in front of an audience.

6. Get legal advice

If you need clarification on copyright law, or find your artwork being used without your permission,
consult a legal professional to ensure your work is sufficiently protected.

7. Fixed in a Tangible Form

The artwork must be fixed in a tangible form, whether it's on paper, canvas, or a digital format. This
means it needs to be expressed in a way that it can be perceived, reproduced, or otherwise communicated

8. NFTs and Copyright

While NFTs (Non-Fungible Tokens) can represent ownership of a digital artwork, they do not transfer the
copyright unless explicitly stated by the artist.

9. Infringement Consequences

Copyright infringement can result in legal action, including fines and damages. In some cases, infringers
may face criminal charges.

10. Attribution

Properly crediting the original artist when using their work is important. Many licenses, including Creative
Commons, require attribution to be given in a specific manner.

11. Derivative Works

If someone creates a new piece of art based on an existing copyrighted work, this new piece is known as a
derivative work. The creator of the derivative work needs permission from the original artist unless the derivative
falls under fair use.

Understanding and strictly adhering to copyright laws is crucial in the digital art realm to avoid harmful legal
consequences. These resources play a pivotal role in clearly comprehending intellectual property rights. By
harnessing the power of these resources, creatives can concentrate more on their artistic output and spend less
time worrying about complex legal nuances.
